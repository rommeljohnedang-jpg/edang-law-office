Adverse Claim on Torrens Title: How to Cancel It in the Philippines

A practical guide to understanding adverse claims under PD 1529, the 30-day rule, and the legal process for cancellation — for property owners, buyers, and heirs.

← Back to Blog Property Law April 7, 2026 Atty. Rommel John G. Edang

Introduction: Understanding the Adverse Claim

If you have recently received notice that an adverse claim has been annotated on your Torrens title, you may feel concerned—and rightfully so. An adverse claim is a warning flag placed on your certificate of title by someone who believes they have a right, interest, or claim over your property. While it does not automatically transfer ownership, it signals a dispute that demands your immediate attention.

Whether you are a property owner facing an unexpected annotation, a buyer about to close a transaction, or an heir navigating inherited property, understanding what an adverse claim is and how to remove it is essential to protecting your property rights in the Philippines.

What Is an Adverse Claim?

An adverse claim is a formal notice registered on a certificate of title under the Torrens system. It indicates that a third party claims to have a legal or equitable interest in the property—such as an unperfected right of ownership, a contractual right, or ownership arising from events that occurred before the Torrens title was issued. The claimant is asserting that their right predates or is superior to the registered owner's title.

The adverse claim serves as a red flag to any prospective buyer or lender: before they acquire or lend money on the property, they should investigate the nature of the claim and ensure it is resolved or waived.

Legal Basis: Section 70 of Presidential Decree No. 1529

The authority to file an adverse claim is found in Section 70 of Presidential Decree No. 1529 (the Property Registration Decree), which is the governing law for the Torrens system in the Philippines. This provision allows any person who believes they have a right or interest in registered property to file an adverse claim, thereby placing a formal annotation on the title. The annotation serves to warn third parties that the property is subject to a dispute or unresolved claim.

Who Can File an Adverse Claim and on What Grounds?

The law does not limit who may file an adverse claim — in theory, anyone can do so. However, the grounds for filing must be substantive. Common grounds include unperfected ownership rights, contractual rights from a deed of sale or agreement, hereditary or succession claims, co-ownership disputes, and equitable or conditional ownership interests.

The Effect of an Adverse Claim on Your Title

When an adverse claim is annotated on your Torrens title, it does not transfer ownership or deprive you of your property. You remain the registered owner. However, the annotation clouds the title — making it unmarketable to buyers and lenders — and may lead to litigation if left unresolved.

The 30-Day Period: What Happens Next?

Under Section 70 of PD 1529, after the adverse claim is annotated, the claimant has 30 days to file an appropriate court action to support the claim. If no action is filed within 30 days, the annotation generally loses its force. However, cancellation is not automatic — the registered owner must petition for removal. If the claimant does file a timely court action, the annotation stays on the title until the case is resolved.

How to Cancel an Adverse Claim: The Legal Process

Under Section 110 of PD 1529, the registered owner may petition the court for cancellation of the adverse claim. The process involves: (1) verifying that the 30-day period has lapsed without a supporting case filed, or that the filed case has been dismissed; (2) filing a formal petition for cancellation; (3) serving the claimant and allowing a response; (4) a court hearing if contested; and (5) the court issuing an order directing the Registry of Deeds to cancel the annotation.

In uncontested cases where the claimant fails to file a supporting action, cancellation can be a relatively swift and low-cost process. In contested cases, however, a full property dispute may ensue requiring documentary evidence and expert legal guidance.

Practical Tips for Property Owners and Claimants

For property owners: act promptly upon learning of an annotation. Consult a lawyer to assess the legitimacy of the claim. If it is baseless, push for cancellation without delay. For claimants: ensure your basis is legally sound before filing — a frivolous adverse claim may expose you to liability. For buyers and lenders: never proceed with a transaction on property bearing an adverse claim without full investigation and resolution.

Conclusion: Protect Your Property Rights

An adverse claim on your Torrens title is a serious matter, but it is manageable with proper legal guidance. Whether the claim is ripe for cancellation or requires negotiation and court proceedings, acting decisively protects your investment and your rights.
